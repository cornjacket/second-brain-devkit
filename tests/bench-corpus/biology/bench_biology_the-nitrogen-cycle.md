# The Nitrogen Cycle and Biogeochemistry

The nitrogen cycle is the biogeochemical circulation of nitrogen through the atmosphere, soil, and living organisms, essential because nitrogen is a constituent of amino acids and nucleic acids yet mostly inaccessible as inert atmospheric dinitrogen gas. Nitrogen fixation converts this gas into ammonia, performed by symbiotic Rhizobium bacteria in the root nodules of legumes and by free-living cyanobacteria bearing the nitrogenase enzyme. Nitrifying bacteria then oxidize ammonia to nitrite and subsequently to nitrate through nitrification, the form most readily assimilated by plant roots. Assimilation incorporates nitrate into organic biomolecules, which pass along the food web to consumers. Decomposers carry out ammonification, releasing ammonia from dead organic matter and excreted urea back into the soil. Finally denitrifying bacteria reduce nitrate to gaseous nitrogen under anaerobic conditions, returning it to the atmosphere and closing the cycle. Human industrial fixation through fertilizer production has substantially perturbed this natural nutrient balance and driven eutrophication.
