# Meiosis and Genetic Recombination

Meiosis is the specialized reductional division that produces haploid gametes from diploid germline cells, halving chromosome number to preserve ploidy across sexual generations. It comprises two sequential divisions after a single round of DNA replication. In prophase I homologous chromosomes pair during synapsis, forming bivalents held by the synaptonemal complex, and reciprocal crossing over at chiasmata exchanges segments between non-sister chromatids, generating genetic recombination. Metaphase I aligns homologous pairs, and anaphase I separates whole homologs through independent assortment, randomizing maternal and paternal chromosomes among daughter cells. Meiosis II resembles mitosis, separating sister chromatids to yield four genetically distinct haploid cells. This recombination and assortment are principal sources of the genetic variation on which evolution acts. Errors in chromosome segregation, called nondisjunction, produce aneuploid gametes underlying conditions such as trisomy, underscoring the importance of accurate homolog disjunction during gametogenesis.
