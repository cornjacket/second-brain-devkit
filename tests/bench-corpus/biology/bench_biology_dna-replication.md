# DNA Replication and the Replication Fork

DNA replication is the semiconservative process by which the double helix is duplicated so each daughter molecule retains one parental template strand. Replication initiates at origins where helicase unwinds the antiparallel strands, single-stranded binding proteins stabilize the exposed nucleotides, and topoisomerase relieves supercoiling ahead of the advancing replication fork. Primase lays down short RNA primers, then DNA polymerase III extends new strands in the five-prime to three-prime direction, reading the template three-prime to five-prime. Because of this directional constraint, the leading strand is synthesized continuously while the lagging strand forms discontinuous Okazaki fragments. DNA polymerase I excises the RNA primers and replaces them with deoxyribonucleotides, and DNA ligase seals the remaining phosphodiester nicks. Proofreading exonuclease activity corrects mispaired bases, ensuring high fidelity of chromosomal duplication before mitotic division through complementary base pairing of adenine, thymine, guanine, and cytosine.
