# Mendelian Genetics and Inheritance

Mendelian genetics describes how discrete hereditary factors, now called genes, transmit traits across generations according to the patterns Gregor Mendel deduced from pea plant crosses. Each diploid organism carries two alleles per locus, and the law of segregation states that these alleles separate during gamete formation so each gamete receives one. The law of independent assortment holds that alleles at different loci sort independently when they reside on separate chromosomes. Dominant alleles mask recessive alleles in the heterozygous genotype, producing predictable phenotypic ratios such as three to one in a monohybrid F2 generation and nine to three to three to one in a dihybrid cross. Punnett squares and test crosses reveal genotypes from phenotypic outcomes. Extensions including incomplete dominance, codominance, multiple alleles, epistasis, and polygenic inheritance elaborate these principles, while pedigree analysis traces autosomal and sex-linked recessive and dominant conditions through successive generations of a family lineage.
