# Natural Selection and Adaptive Evolution

Natural selection is the differential survival and reproduction of organisms whose heritable phenotypic traits confer greater fitness within a given environment. Darwin and Wallace recognized that populations produce more offspring than can survive, that individuals vary heritably, and that this variation influences reproductive success, so advantageous alleles increase in frequency over generations. Directional selection shifts a trait distribution toward one extreme, stabilizing selection favors intermediate phenotypes, and disruptive selection favors both extremes over the mean. Sexual selection acts through mate choice and intrasexual competition, sculpting elaborate ornaments and dimorphism. Over deep evolutionary time these mechanisms yield adaptations exquisitely matched to ecological niches, from cryptic camouflage to antibiotic resistance in bacterial populations. Selection operates on existing genetic variation supplied by mutation and recombination, and its cumulative effect, filtered through the environment across countless generations, produces the descent with modification that unites all lineages in a branching phylogenetic tree of common ancestry.
