# Speciation and Reproductive Isolation

Speciation is the evolutionary process by which one ancestral population diverges into two or more distinct species that can no longer interbreed. Under the biological species concept, speciation is complete when reproductive isolating barriers prevent gene flow between diverging populations. Allopatric speciation arises when a geographic barrier physically separates populations, allowing genetic drift, mutation, and divergent natural selection to accumulate differences until reproductive incompatibility evolves. Sympatric speciation occurs without geographic separation, driven by mechanisms such as polyploidy in plants, disruptive selection, or exploitation of distinct ecological niches. Prezygotic isolating mechanisms, including temporal, behavioral, mechanical, and gametic barriers, prevent mating or fertilization, while postzygotic mechanisms such as hybrid inviability and hybrid sterility reduce hybrid fitness. Adaptive radiation rapidly generates many descendant species from a common ancestor colonizing diverse habitats, exemplified by Darwin's finches. Over macroevolutionary time these divergences produce the branching phylogeny and immense biodiversity documented in the fossil record and molecular data.
