# The Microbiome and Host-Microbe Symbiosis

The microbiome is the vast community of microorganisms, chiefly bacteria along with archaea, fungi, and viruses, that colonize the surfaces and cavities of a host organism in symbiotic association. The human gut harbors trillions of commensal microbes whose collective microbial genome, the metagenome, vastly exceeds the host's own gene complement. These intestinal communities ferment dietary fiber into short-chain fatty acids that nourish colonic epithelial cells, synthesize vitamins, and outcompete pathogenic invaders through colonization resistance. The microbiota educates and modulates the developing immune system, shaping mucosal tolerance and inflammatory balance, and signals along the gut-brain axis. Dysbiosis, a disruption of microbial community composition and diversity often following antibiotic exposure, is associated with metabolic, immune, and inflammatory disorders. Characterized through sixteen S ribosomal RNA sequencing and shotgun metagenomics, the microbiome exemplifies mutualistic coevolution in which host and microbial partners reciprocally influence each other's physiology, ecology, and evolutionary trajectory across the lifespan.
