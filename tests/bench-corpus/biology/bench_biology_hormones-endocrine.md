# Hormones and the Endocrine System

The endocrine system regulates physiology through hormones, chemical messengers secreted by ductless glands into the bloodstream to act on distant target tissues bearing specific receptors. The hypothalamus links neural and endocrine control, directing the pituitary gland, whose anterior lobe releases growth hormone, thyroid-stimulating hormone, and gonadotropins, and whose posterior lobe stores antidiuretic hormone and oxytocin. Steroid hormones derived from cholesterol, such as cortisol and the sex steroids estrogen and testosterone, diffuse across the plasma membrane to bind intracellular receptors that modulate gene transcription. Peptide and amine hormones like insulin and adrenaline bind cell-surface receptors, activating second-messenger cascades. The thyroid gland secretes thyroxine to set metabolic rate, the adrenal glands mediate stress responses, and the pancreatic islets tune blood glucose. Hormone secretion is governed by negative feedback along axes such as the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis, maintaining endocrine equilibrium and coordinating growth, metabolism, reproduction, and the stress response across the whole organism.
