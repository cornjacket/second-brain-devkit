# Taxonomy and Biological Classification

Taxonomy is the science of naming, describing, and classifying organisms into a nested hierarchy reflecting evolutionary relationships. Carl Linnaeus established binomial nomenclature, assigning each species a two-part Latin name of genus and specific epithet, and organized life into ranked taxa: domain, kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species. Modern systematics places organisms within three domains, the Bacteria and Archaea comprising prokaryotes and the Eukarya encompassing protists, fungi, plants, and animals. Cladistics groups taxa into monophyletic clades defined by shared derived characters inherited from a common ancestor, and phylogenetic trees inferred from morphology and molecular sequence data depict these lineages of descent. A species is often delimited by the biological species concept as a group of interbreeding populations reproductively isolated from others. Homologous structures indicate common ancestry while analogous traits arise through convergent evolution, and molecular phylogenetics increasingly resolves deep branching patterns among the diverse lineages of life.
