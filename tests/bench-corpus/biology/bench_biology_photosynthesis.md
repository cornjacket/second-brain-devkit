# Photosynthesis and Carbon Fixation

Photosynthesis converts light energy into chemical energy within chloroplasts, storing it in the bonds of carbohydrate. In the light-dependent reactions on the thylakoid membranes, photosystem II and photosystem I absorb photons through chlorophyll pigments, exciting electrons that pass down an electron transport chain. Water is photolyzed to release oxygen, protons, and electrons, while the resulting proton gradient across the thylakoid drives ATP synthase to produce ATP, and NADP is reduced to NADPH. These energy carriers fuel the light-independent Calvin cycle in the stroma, where the enzyme RuBisCO fixes atmospheric carbon dioxide onto ribulose bisphosphate. The resulting three-carbon intermediates are reduced and rearranged to regenerate the acceptor and yield glyceraldehyde phosphate, precursor to glucose. C4 and CAM plants employ spatial and temporal adaptations to minimize photorespiration under high temperature and low stomatal conductance, optimizing carbon assimilation in arid environments.
