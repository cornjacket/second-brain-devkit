# Cellular Respiration and ATP Synthesis

Cellular respiration catabolizes glucose to harvest chemical energy as ATP through three coupled stages. Glycolysis in the cytosol splits glucose into two pyruvate molecules, yielding a net two ATP and two NADH via substrate-level phosphorylation. Under aerobic conditions pyruvate is decarboxylated to acetyl coenzyme A, which enters the citric acid cycle in the mitochondrial matrix, where successive oxidations reduce NAD and FAD to NADH and FADH2 while releasing carbon dioxide. These electron carriers deliver high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain on the inner mitochondrial membrane, where complexes pump protons into the intermembrane space, establishing a chemiosmotic proton-motive force. ATP synthase harnesses this electrochemical gradient to phosphorylate ADP through oxidative phosphorylation, with molecular oxygen serving as the terminal electron acceptor to form water. When oxygen is scarce, cells revert to anaerobic fermentation, regenerating NAD to sustain glycolytic flux and metabolic homeostasis.
