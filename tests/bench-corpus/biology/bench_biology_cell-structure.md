# Eukaryotic Cell Structure and Organelles

The eukaryotic cell is bounded by a phospholipid bilayer plasma membrane studded with integral and peripheral membrane proteins that regulate selective permeability and transmembrane transport. Inside the cytoplasm, membrane-bound organelles partition biochemical functions: the nucleus houses chromatin and the nucleolus, the rough endoplasmic reticulum bears ribosomes for cotranslational protein folding, and the smooth endoplasmic reticulum synthesizes lipids and detoxifies compounds. The Golgi apparatus glycosylates and packages secretory vesicles, while mitochondria carry their own circular genome and cristae-rich inner membranes. Lysosomes contain acid hydrolases for autophagy, peroxisomes oxidize fatty acids, and the cytoskeleton of microtubules, actin microfilaments, and intermediate filaments maintains cell shape and enables intracellular trafficking. Plant cells additionally possess a cellulose cell wall, a large central vacuole maintaining turgor pressure, and chloroplasts, distinguishing them structurally from prokaryotic cells that lack any membrane-enclosed nucleus.
