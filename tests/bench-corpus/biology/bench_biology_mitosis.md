# Mitosis and the Cell Cycle

Mitosis is the process of nuclear division that partitions duplicated chromosomes equally into two genetically identical diploid daughter cells. It follows interphase, during which the cell grows and replicates its DNA in S phase, bracketed by the G1 and G2 growth gaps and monitored by cell-cycle checkpoints. In prophase the replicated sister chromatids condense and the mitotic spindle of microtubules assembles from the centrosomes. During prometaphase the nuclear envelope breaks down and kinetochore microtubules attach to centromeres. Metaphase aligns chromosomes along the equatorial metaphase plate, and anaphase separates sister chromatids toward opposite spindle poles as cohesin is cleaved. Telophase reforms nuclear envelopes around the two decondensing chromosome sets, and cytokinesis cleaves the cytoplasm through a contractile actin ring in animal cells or a cell plate in plants. This faithful segregation underlies tissue growth, regeneration, and asexual reproduction while preserving chromosome number.
