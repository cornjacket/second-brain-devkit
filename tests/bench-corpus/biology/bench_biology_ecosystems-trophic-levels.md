# Ecosystems and Trophic Levels

An ecosystem comprises the biotic community of organisms interacting with their abiotic physical environment through flows of energy and cycling of matter. Energy enters as sunlight captured by autotrophic primary producers, chiefly photosynthetic plants and phytoplankton, which form the base of the food web. Herbivorous primary consumers graze on producers, secondary and tertiary consumers prey upon them, and apex predators occupy the highest trophic level. At each transfer only about ten percent of the energy in one trophic level is assimilated into the next, the remainder lost as metabolic heat, so ecological pyramids of energy and biomass taper sharply upward and constrain food chain length. Decomposers and detritivores such as fungi and bacteria break down dead organic matter, recycling nutrients back to producers. Interactions of predation, competition, mutualism, and parasitism structure community composition, while keystone species exert disproportionate influence and biodiversity confers resilience to the ecosystem against disturbance.
