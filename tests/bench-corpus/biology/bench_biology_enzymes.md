# Enzymes and Biological Catalysis

Enzymes are biological catalysts, predominantly globular proteins, that accelerate biochemical reactions by lowering activation energy without being consumed. Each enzyme binds its substrate at an active site whose complementary shape and chemistry confer exquisite specificity, described by the induced-fit model in which the site molds around the bound substrate to stabilize the transition state. Catalytic rate depends on substrate concentration following Michaelis-Menten kinetics, characterized by the maximal velocity and the Michaelis constant reflecting substrate affinity. Cofactors and organic coenzymes assist catalysis, while temperature and pH shift enzyme activity, and extremes denature the tertiary conformation. Enzyme activity is regulated by competitive inhibitors that occupy the active site and noncompetitive inhibitors that bind allosteric sites, and by feedback inhibition in which a pathway's end product downregulates an upstream enzyme. Allosteric activators and covalent modification such as phosphorylation further modulate flux, allowing metabolic pathways to respond dynamically to cellular demand and maintain biochemical equilibrium within the cytoplasm.
