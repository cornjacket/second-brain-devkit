# The Immune System and Host Defense

The immune system defends the organism against pathogens through layered innate and adaptive responses. Innate immunity provides immediate nonspecific defense via physical epithelial barriers, phagocytic macrophages and neutrophils, natural killer cells, the complement cascade, and inflammatory signaling by cytokines that recruit leukocytes to infected tissue. Adaptive immunity mounts a slower antigen-specific response mediated by lymphocytes. B cells differentiate into plasma cells secreting antibodies that neutralize antigens and opsonize microbes for phagocytosis, constituting humoral immunity. T cells orchestrate cell-mediated immunity: helper T cells recognize antigens presented on major histocompatibility complex molecules and secrete cytokines to activate other leukocytes, while cytotoxic T cells destroy virus-infected and malignant cells. Clonal selection expands lymphocytes bearing receptors specific to the invading antigen, and memory cells persist to confer lasting immunological memory, the basis of vaccination. Self-tolerance prevents autoimmunity, and dysregulation produces allergy, hypersensitivity, or immunodeficiency compromising host survival.
