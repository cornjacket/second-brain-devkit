# Homeostasis and Physiological Regulation

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal physiological environment despite fluctuating external conditions, achieved through negative feedback loops that counteract deviations from a set point. A regulatory pathway comprises a sensor detecting the controlled variable, an integrating control center comparing it to the set point, and effectors that restore equilibrium. Thermoregulation illustrates this: when core body temperature rises, thermoreceptors signal the hypothalamus to trigger vasodilation and sweating, dissipating heat, whereas cooling elicits vasoconstruction, shivering thermogenesis, and piloerection. Osmoregulation balances water and solute concentrations through antidiuretic hormone acting on renal nephrons, and blood glucose is stabilized by the antagonistic action of insulin and glucagon on hepatic glycogen. Blood pH is buffered near physiological neutrality by the bicarbonate system and respiratory and renal compensation. Positive feedback loops, though less common, amplify processes such as parturition. Together these homeostatic mechanisms preserve the constant milieu interieur that cellular metabolism requires to function reliably.
