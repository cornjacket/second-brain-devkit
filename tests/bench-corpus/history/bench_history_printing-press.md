# Gutenberg and the Printing Revolution

Around 1440 in Mainz, the goldsmith Johannes Gutenberg perfected printing with movable metal type, casting individual letters in a hand mold from an alloy of lead, tin, and antimony and inking them with an oil-based formula pressed onto paper by an adapted screw press. His masterpiece, the forty-two-line Gutenberg Bible of 1455, demonstrated the technology's power. Financed and later sued by his backer Johann Fust, Gutenberg saw his workshop pass to Peter Schöffer. Printing spread rapidly through the Rhineland to Venice, where Aldus Manutius produced portable octavo editions and italic type, and across Europe, producing incunabula by the millions before 1501. The proliferation of printed books slashed the cost of texts, standardized vernacular languages, accelerated the spread of humanist scholarship, and later carried Reformation pamphlets, permanently transforming how knowledge was reproduced and disseminated across the continent.
