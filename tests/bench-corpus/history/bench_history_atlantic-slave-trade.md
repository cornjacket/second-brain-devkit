# The Transatlantic Slave Trade

The transatlantic slave trade forcibly transported roughly twelve million enslaved Africans across the Atlantic between the sixteenth and nineteenth centuries in the horrors of the Middle Passage. European traders, first the Portuguese and Spanish, later the Dutch, English, and French, operated a triangular commerce exchanging manufactured goods, firearms, and rum on the West African coast for captives sold by kingdoms like Dahomey, Ashanti, and Kongo. Chained below decks in slave ships departing forts like Elmina and Cape Coast Castle, captives endured disease and appalling mortality en route to the plantations of Brazil, the Caribbean, and the American South. There they labored on sugar, tobacco, and cotton estates under brutal chattel bondage. Abolitionists such as Olaudah Equiano, William Wilberforce, and Thomas Clarkson campaigned against the trade, which Britain outlawed in 1807, though slavery itself persisted for decades more across the Americas.
