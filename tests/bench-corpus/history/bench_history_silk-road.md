# The Silk Road

The Silk Road was a sprawling network of overland caravan routes linking Han China to the Mediterranean across Central Asia, flourishing from the second century BCE under the Han emperor Wu, whose envoy Zhang Qian opened contact with the western regions. Camel caravans carried Chinese silk, porcelain, and lacquerware westward through Dunhuang, the Taklamakan oases of Kashgar and Samarkand, and Persia, returning with horses, glassware, wool, and precious metals. Merchants like the Sogdians brokered the trade, while cities such as Bukhara and Merv grew wealthy as caravanserais sheltered travelers. Beyond goods, the routes transmitted Buddhism from India into China, along with Nestorian Christianity, Manichaeism, and later Islam, as well as technologies and diseases. The Pax Mongolica under Genghis Khan and his successors revived the network in the thirteenth century, enabling travelers like Marco Polo to journey the length of Eurasia.
