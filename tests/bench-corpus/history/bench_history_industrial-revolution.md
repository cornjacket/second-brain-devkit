# The British Industrial Revolution

The Industrial Revolution transformed Britain from an agrarian into a manufacturing economy between roughly 1760 and 1840, driven by mechanized textile production, coal, and steam power. In Lancashire cotton mills, inventions like Hargreaves's spinning jenny, Arkwright's water frame, Crompton's mule, and Cartwright's power loom multiplied output, while Eli Whitney's cotton gin fed raw fiber from across the Atlantic. James Watt's improved steam engine, condensed from Newcomen's design, powered factories and, through Stephenson's Rocket, the railways. Abraham Darby's coke-smelting and Henry Cort's puddling process revolutionized iron. Rural laborers migrated into grimy manufacturing towns like Manchester and Birmingham, enduring long shifts, child labor, and squalid tenements documented by reformers. Canals and turnpikes gave way to rail networks. This surge of mechanization, factory discipline, and urbanization, underpinned by abundant capital and coal, inaugurated sustained economic growth and the modern industrial order.
