# The Fall of the Western Roman Empire

The Western Roman Empire disintegrated over the fifth century CE amid barbarian incursions, fiscal exhaustion, and dynastic instability. After Diocletian's tetrarchy and Constantine's founding of Constantinople, the empire split administratively into eastern and western halves. Pressure from the Huns under Attila drove Germanic peoples across the Rhine and Danube frontiers; the Visigoths under Alaric sacked Rome in 410 CE, and the Vandals under Gaiseric plundered it again in 455. Emperors like Honorius and Valentinian III ruled feebly from Ravenna while generals such as Stilicho and Aetius wielded real power. The field army thinned as foederati tribes settled within the provinces. In 476 CE the Germanic commander Odoacer deposed the boy-emperor Romulus Augustulus, conventionally ending Western imperial rule, while the Eastern Empire endured from Constantinople as what later historians would call Byzantium.
