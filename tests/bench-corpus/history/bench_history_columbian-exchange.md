# The Columbian Exchange

The Columbian Exchange denotes the vast transfer of plants, animals, peoples, and pathogens between the Old World and the Americas following Christopher Columbus's 1492 voyage under Castilian sponsorship. Spanish conquistadors like Hernán Cortés and Francisco Pizarro toppled the Aztec and Inca empires, aided catastrophically by smallpox, measles, and influenza that devastated indigenous populations lacking immunity. Eastward flowed American crops that transformed global diets: maize, potatoes, tomatoes, cacao, tobacco, and chili peppers. Westward came wheat, sugarcane, coffee, horses, cattle, pigs, and the Iberian encomienda system. Silver from Potosí and Zacatecas flooded into the Ming economy through Manila galleons. This biological and ecological convergence, chronicled by figures like Bartolomé de las Casas, reshaped demography and agriculture across four continents, enriching Habsburg Spain while inaugurating centuries of colonization, forced labor, and the linking of hemispheric ecosystems.
