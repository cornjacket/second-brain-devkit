# The Origins of the First World War

The First World War erupted in 1914 from a combustible mix of entangling alliances, militarism, imperial rivalry, and nationalism that had accumulated across Europe for decades. The continent was divided between the Triple Alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy and the Triple Entente of France, Russia, and Britain. Kaiser Wilhelm II's naval arms race with Britain, the Franco-German antagonism over Alsace-Lorraine, and Balkan instability heightened tensions. The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand by the Serbian nationalist Gavrilo Princip in Sarajevo on the twenty-eighth of June 1914 triggered the July Crisis. Austria-Hungary's ultimatum to Serbia, backed by Germany's blank cheque, set the alliance system cascading: Russia mobilized, Germany invaded neutral Belgium under the Schlieffen Plan, and Britain declared war. Rigid mobilization timetables and diplomatic miscalculation transformed a regional quarrel into a devastating continental conflagration.
