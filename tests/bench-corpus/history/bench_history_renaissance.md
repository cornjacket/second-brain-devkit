# The Italian Renaissance

The Renaissance flowered in the Italian city-states from the fourteenth through sixteenth centuries as a revival of classical antiquity and humanist learning. In Florence, the Medici banking dynasty patronized artists and scholars who recovered Greek and Latin texts, championing studia humanitatis over scholastic theology. Petrarch and the humanist Poggio Bracciolini hunted forgotten manuscripts, while Brunelleschi engineered the great dome of Santa Maria del Fiore and rediscovered linear perspective. Painters and sculptors including Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Raphael, and Botticelli fused naturalism with classical ideals, producing the frescoes of the Sistine Chapel and the marble David. Machiavelli dissected statecraft in The Prince, and Alberti embodied the universal man. The movement radiated from Florence to Rome and Venice, transforming architecture, painting, and civic ideals, and spread northward through commerce and the printed word to reshape European intellectual life.
