# The Black Death and the Great Pestilence

The Black Death, a pandemic of plague caused by Yersinia pestis, swept across Eurasia between 1347 and 1351, killing perhaps a third of Europe's population. Carried by fleas on black rats aboard Genoese galleys fleeing the siege of Caffa in the Crimea, the contagion reached Sicily, then Marseille, Florence, Paris, and London. Chroniclers like the Florentine Giovanni Boccaccio, whose Decameron framed the horror, described buboes, fever, and swift death. Flagellant processions scourged themselves in penance, while terrified townsfolk blamed and massacred Jewish communities in pogroms across the Rhineland. Physicians steeped in Galenic medicine and miasma theory proved helpless. The demographic catastrophe shattered the manorial economy, raised peasant wages, spurred revolts like the English Peasants' Revolt of 1381, and loosened the bonds of serfdom, reshaping late medieval society for generations.
