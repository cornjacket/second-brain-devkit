# The Age of Enlightenment

The Enlightenment was an eighteenth-century intellectual movement exalting reason, empiricism, and natural law, rooted in the scientific revolution of Newton and the philosophy of Locke and Descartes. In Parisian salons and coffeehouses, the philosophes advanced skepticism toward monarchy, aristocracy, and the Catholic clergy. Voltaire assailed intolerance, Montesquieu theorized the separation of powers in The Spirit of the Laws, and Rousseau explored the social contract and the general will. Denis Diderot and Jean d'Alembert compiled the monumental Encyclopédie to catalog human knowledge. Across the Channel, Scottish thinkers like David Hume and Adam Smith advanced moral philosophy and political economy. Enlightened absolutist rulers such as Frederick the Great of Prussia and Catherine the Great of Russia courted the philosophes. These ideas of liberty, progress, and reasoned inquiry circulated through pamphlets and correspondence, seeding the revolutionary upheavals that soon followed.
