# Feudalism in Medieval Europe

Feudalism structured medieval European society through reciprocal bonds of vassalage, fief, and homage that emerged after the Carolingian collapse and the Viking, Magyar, and Saracen raids of the ninth and tenth centuries. A lord granted a vassal a fief, typically land worked by serfs, in exchange for military service, counsel, and the aid owed by a knight. The manor formed the economic core, where villeins performed labor dues, paid banalities at the lord's mill and oven, and rendered a tithe to the parish church. The chivalric code, castles with motte-and-bailey and later stone keeps, and the ceremony of dubbing bound the warrior aristocracy. Kings like the Capetians and the Norman conqueror William granted honors to tenants-in-chief through subinfeudation. This decentralized order of seigneurial obligation gradually eroded as towns, money rents, and royal administration reasserted centralized authority.
