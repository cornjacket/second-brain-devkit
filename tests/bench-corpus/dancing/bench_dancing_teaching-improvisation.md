# Teaching improvisation

Improvisation is taught not as random moving but as trained spontaneous composition, and it develops a dancer's movement invention and presence. Teachers use structured scores — prompts, constraints, or tasks such as moving only from the joints, mirroring a partner, or responding to a single image — so students explore within a frame rather than freeze or fall back on habits. The aim is to sharpen listening: to the body's impulses, to the space, and to the other dancers, making real-time choices about level, speed, direction, and stillness. Coaches encourage students to stay with an idea and let it develop instead of switching constantly, and to treat surprises as material rather than mistakes. Improvisation feeds choreography and performance by building comfort with the unplanned, teaching dancers to keep moving through the unexpected instead of stopping.
