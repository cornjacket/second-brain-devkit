# Teaching turnout and placement

Turnout — rotating the legs outward from the hip so the knees and toes face away from center — is one of the first things a ballet teacher builds and one of the most misunderstood. Beginners force it from the feet, which torques the knees and rolls the arches; teach them instead to find the rotation from the deep external rotators of the hip and to use only the range they can control. At the barre, coach placement first: a lifted core, shoulders stacked over hips, weight centered over the balls of the feet. Drill turnout through pliés and tendus where alignment is easy to watch, and cue students to hold the standing leg's rotation as the working leg moves. Honest turnout grows slowly with strength; chasing a flat 180 degrees before the body is ready only invites knee and ankle injury.
