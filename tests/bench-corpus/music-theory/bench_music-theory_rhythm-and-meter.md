# Rhythm and Meter

Rhythm is the organization of sound and silence in time, built from durations such as whole, half, quarter, eighth, and sixteenth notes together with their corresponding rests, while meter is the recurring pattern of strong and weak beats that groups those durations. A steady underlying pulse, the beat, is subdivided and grouped into measures marked by barlines, and the downbeat receives the strongest metric accent. Simple meters divide each beat into two, compound meters divide each beat into three, and the interplay of accents creates the felt weight of duple, triple, or quadruple groupings. Syncopation displaces expected accents onto weak beats or offbeats, generating momentum against the pulse, while tuplets like triplets subdivide a beat into unequal portions. Ties, dots, and beaming clarify how durations span or fill beats, and tempo, measured in beats per minute, sets the overall pace at which the rhythmic fabric unfolds.
