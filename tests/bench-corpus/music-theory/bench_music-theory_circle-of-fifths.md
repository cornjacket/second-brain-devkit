# The Circle of Fifths

The circle of fifths arranges the twelve pitch classes clockwise by ascending perfect fifths, so C leads to G, D, A, and onward until the sequence closes back on itself after twelve steps through enharmonic equivalence. Moving clockwise adds one sharp to the key signature at each station, while moving counterclockwise by descending fifths adds one flat, making the diagram a map of tonal relationships and signature counts. Adjacent keys are closely related, sharing all but one pitch, which is why modulation between neighbors sounds smooth. The circle also pairs each major key with its relative minor on an inner ring and highlights the dominant-to-tonic pull that drives functional harmony, since the dominant always lies one step clockwise. Composers use it to plan progressions, chart pivot chords, and understand why the subdominant and dominant flank the tonic as its nearest harmonic neighbors.
