# Time Signatures

A time signature is the pair of stacked numbers placed after the clef and key signature that defines how beats are grouped within each measure. The upper number states how many beats fill a measure, and the lower number names the note value that receives one beat, so four-four means four quarter-note beats and six-eight means six eighth-note beats. Simple meters like two-four, three-four, and four-four divide each beat into two equal parts, while compound meters like six-eight, nine-eight, and twelve-eight divide each beat into three, felt as a dotted-note pulse. Asymmetric or irregular signatures such as five-four and seven-eight group beats into uneven clusters, and the common-time and cut-time symbols abbreviate four-four and two-two. The time signature governs barline placement, beaming, and metric accent, telling the performer where the downbeat falls and how the rhythmic subdivisions should be counted and grouped across the measure.
