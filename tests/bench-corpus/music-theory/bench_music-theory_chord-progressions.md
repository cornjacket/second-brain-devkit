# Chord Progressions

A chord progression is a purposeful succession of harmonies that propels a passage forward and establishes its key, most often described with Roman numerals so the pattern transposes to any tonality. The tonic, predominant, and dominant functions organize motion, with the archetypal I-IV-V-I framing countless tonal works and the ii-V-I supplying the backbone of jazz harmony. Root motion by descending fifth sounds strongest, which is why circle-of-fifths sequences feel inevitable, while stepwise and third-related motions offer gentler color. Popular music leans on the I-V-vi-IV loop and the twelve-bar blues built from tonic, subdominant, and dominant seventh chords. Cadences punctuate progressions, deceptive resolutions substitute the submediant for an expected tonic, and secondary dominants borrow the dominant of a nearby degree to intensify the pull. Voice leading and harmonic rhythm then determine how convincingly one chord flows into the next.
