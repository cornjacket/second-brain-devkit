# Intervals

An interval measures the distance in pitch between two notes, named by a numeric size counting staff positions and a quality that refines it. Unisons, fourths, fifths, and octaves take the qualities perfect, augmented, or diminished, while seconds, thirds, sixths, and sevenths are major, minor, augmented, or diminished. A major third spans four semitones and a minor third three, so lowering a major interval by a half step yields the minor, and lowering it further yields the diminished. Intervals are consonant or dissonant: perfect fifths and thirds sound stable, whereas the tritone, three whole tones bridging a diminished fifth or augmented fourth, sounds tense and demands resolution. Inverting an interval sums to nine, so a third inverts to a sixth and a perfect fifth to a perfect fourth. Compound intervals exceed the octave, and enharmonic spelling can rename the same sounding distance.
