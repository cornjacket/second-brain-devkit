# Seventh Chords

A seventh chord extends a triad by stacking one more third above the fifth, adding the seventh scale degree measured from the chord root and enriching the harmony with a mild dissonance that seeks resolution. Five common types arise from combining triad qualities with major or minor sevenths: the major seventh pairs a major triad with a major seventh, the dominant seventh pairs a major triad with a minor seventh, the minor seventh pairs a minor triad with a minor seventh, the half-diminished pairs a diminished triad with a minor seventh, and the fully diminished stacks three minor thirds. The dominant seventh is pivotal because its tritone between the third and seventh resolves inward to the tonic, defining functional cadence. Inversions produce figured-bass symbols seven, six-five, four-three, and four-two, and jazz notation labels these chords with symbols like maj7, m7, and 7 for improvisation.
