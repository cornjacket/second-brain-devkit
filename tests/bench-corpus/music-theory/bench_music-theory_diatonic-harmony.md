# Diatonic Harmony

Diatonic harmony describes the chords formed by stacking thirds exclusively on the seven notes of a given major or minor scale, yielding a fixed palette of triads whose qualities follow the scale's interval pattern. In a major key the triads run major, minor, minor, major, major, minor, and diminished on degrees one through seven, giving the Roman numerals I, ii, iii, IV, V, vi, and vii diminished. These chords sort into tonic, predominant, and dominant functions that govern harmonic motion, with the tonic offering rest, the dominant demanding resolution through its leading tone, and the predominant preparing that dominant. Because every chord draws only from the parent scale, diatonic passages sound unified and firmly in key. Departures from this collection, such as borrowed chords, secondary dominants, or chromatic alterations, gain their expressive charge precisely by contrasting with the diatonic norm that listeners internalize as home.
