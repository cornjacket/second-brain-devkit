# Triads and Chords

A triad is a three-note chord built by stacking two thirds above a root, producing four qualities distinguished by the size of those thirds. A major triad stacks a major third then a minor third, a minor triad reverses the order, a diminished triad stacks two minor thirds spanning a diminished fifth, and an augmented triad stacks two major thirds spanning an augmented fifth. The lowest sounding note determines inversion: root position places the root in the bass, first inversion places the third, and second inversion places the fifth, notated in figured bass as six-three and six-four respectively. Chords are labeled by Roman numerals reflecting the scale degree of their root, uppercase for major and lowercase for minor, so the tonic, subdominant, and dominant triads frame diatonic harmony. Doubling, spacing, and voicing then determine how the chord is realized across registers.
