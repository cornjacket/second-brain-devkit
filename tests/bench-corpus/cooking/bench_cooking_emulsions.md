# Sauce emulsions

An emulsion suspends droplets of one liquid inside another that normally won't mix — oil in water for mayonnaise and vinaigrette, water in fat for hollandaise and beurre blanc. It holds because an emulsifier coats the droplets and stops them coalescing; egg-yolk lecithin is the classic one, and mustard adds both flavor and stabilizing power. Technique comes down to droplet size: add the oil in a thin stream while whisking hard so the shear breaks it fine, and never rush the fat or the sauce breaks. A broken mayonnaise can be rescued by whisking the split mixture drop by drop into a fresh yolk. Temperature matters for the warm emulsions — hollandaise curdles if the yolks pass roughly 160°F, so work off direct heat over a gentle bain-marie and pull it before it tightens.
