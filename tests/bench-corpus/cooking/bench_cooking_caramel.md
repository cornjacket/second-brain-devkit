# Caramel and sugar work

Caramelization is sugar cooked until it browns and develops hundreds of new bittersweet, toasty compounds — distinct from the Maillard reaction because no proteins are involved, only sugar breaking down under heat. Melt the sugar slowly, either dry in a pan or wet with a little water, watching closely because it races from pale gold to amber to acrid black in seconds once it takes off. A wet caramel is more forgiving but risks crystallizing if stray sugar crystals cling to the pan sides, so brush them down with water or add a little acid or glucose to interrupt the crystal lattice. Stopping the cook is decisive: pull it a shade early and carryover finishes the browning, or add cream and butter for a sauce, standing back as it seizes and steams violently before smoothing into silk.
