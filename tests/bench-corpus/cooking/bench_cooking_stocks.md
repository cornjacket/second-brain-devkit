# Stocks and broth

A good stock is the backbone of the kitchen, extracting flavor, body, and gelatin from bones, aromatics, and time. Roast the bones first for a deep brown stock or use them raw for a pale one, then cover with cold water and bring it up slowly so albumin proteins rise as a scum you can skim rather than cloud the pot. A mirepoix of onion, carrot, and celery plus a bouquet garni of thyme, bay, and parsley stems rounds it out; hold a bare simmer, never a boil, for hours so collagen converts to gelatin without churning the fat back in. Strain through a fine chinois, cool it fast in an ice bath to stay food-safe, and reduce for a concentrated demi-glace. Never salt a stock you plan to reduce later, or it turns briny.
