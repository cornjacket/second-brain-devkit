# Bread fermentation

Bread rises because yeast ferments the flour's sugars, exhaling carbon dioxide that inflates a gluten network into an airy crumb. A sourdough starter is a living culture of wild yeast and lactobacilli kept alive by regular feeding of flour and water; the bacteria produce lactic and acetic acid, which is where the tang comes from. Bulk fermentation is the long first rise, where flavor and structure develop, and folding the dough during it builds strength without kneading. Shaping creates surface tension so the loaf holds its form, then a cold overnight proof in the fridge deepens flavor and eases scheduling. Bake with steam for the first minutes so the crust stays supple and the loaf springs tall, then dry heat sets a deep, crackling crust. Watch hydration and dough temperature; both quietly govern the whole process.
