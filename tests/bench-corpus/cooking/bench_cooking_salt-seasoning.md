# Salt and seasoning

Salt is the single most important seasoning because it does not just taste salty — it suppresses bitterness, heightens aroma, and makes other flavors legible. Season in layers throughout cooking rather than only at the end, so the salt penetrates instead of sitting on the surface; salting meat well ahead acts as a dry brine, drawing out moisture that then reabsorbs as a seasoned, tenderized surface. Different salts behave differently: fine table salt is dense and easy to over-pour, while flaky kosher or Maldon salt is more forgiving and finishes with a crunch. Taste constantly and adjust, remembering that a dish reducing on the stove will concentrate its salt as the water leaves. When something tastes flat but you have salted enough, a hit of acid often supplies the brightness that salt alone cannot, and a pinch of sugar can round sharp edges.
