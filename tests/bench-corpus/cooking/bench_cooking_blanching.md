# Blanching and shocking

Blanching briefly boils vegetables then plunges them into ice water to shock them, locking in color, texture, and nutrients. The rolling salted water should be generous so it returns to a boil quickly when the vegetables go in; a fast, short cook sets the bright chlorophyll green before the acids released by heat can dull it to olive. The ice bath halts carryover cooking instantly, so green beans or broccoli stay crisp-tender rather than going soft and drab. Blanching also loosens tomato and peach skins for peeling, tames the raw bite of onions, and par-cooks dense vegetables so they finish evenly in a quick saute later. Drain and dry blanched vegetables well before storing or they weep and dilute whatever they meet. It is a prep technique as much as a cooking one, and it underpins clean, vibrant plates.
