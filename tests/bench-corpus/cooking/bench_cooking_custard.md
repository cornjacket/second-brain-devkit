# Custards

A custard thickens a liquid with egg proteins that gently coagulate into a smooth, spoonable set — the basis of crème anglaise, pastry cream, quiche, and flan. The whole game is gentle, even heat: eggs begin to set around 150°F and scramble past roughly 180°F, so a stirred custard is cooked slowly over low heat or a bain-marie until it coats the back of a spoon and holds a line drawn through it. Tempering — whisking a little hot liquid into the eggs before combining the two — keeps them from curdling on contact. Starch, as in pastry cream, stabilizes the mixture and lets it come to a boil without breaking. A baked custard sits in a water bath so its edges do not overheat and weep before the center just sets with a faint jiggle at the middle.
