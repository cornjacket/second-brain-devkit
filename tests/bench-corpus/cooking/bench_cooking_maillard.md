# The Maillard reaction

The Maillard reaction is the browning that gives a seared steak, toast, roasted coffee, and fried onions their deep, savory flavor. It is a cascade of reactions between amino acids and reducing sugars that kicks off meaningfully above about 300°F, which is why a wet surface stalls it — the food must shed its water before the pan can climb past the boiling point. Pat proteins dry, don't crowd the pan, and let a crust form before flipping. It is distinct from caramelization, which is sugars browning alone without amino acids. The reaction generates hundreds of new aroma compounds along with a brown pigment called melanoidin. Push it too far and bitter, acrid notes take over, so browning is a narrow window you ride between pale and burnt — control it with heat, time, and a dry surface.
