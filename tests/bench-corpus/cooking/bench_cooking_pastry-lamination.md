# Laminated pastry

Lamination is the technique behind croissants and puff pastry, building dozens of alternating layers of dough and butter that steam apart in the oven into a shatteringly flaky rise. You enclose a slab of cold butter in a dough envelope, then repeatedly roll and fold — the "turns" — each one multiplying the layer count. The entire craft is temperature control: the butter must stay cold yet pliable so it sheets between the dough rather than melting in or cracking through, which means resting the dough in the fridge between turns. In the oven the water in the butter flashes to steam and pushes the layers apart while the gluten sets around them, and an egg wash browns the exterior. Rush the chilling and the butter melts into the dough, welding the layers so the pastry bakes dense instead of airy.
