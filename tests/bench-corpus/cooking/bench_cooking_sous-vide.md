# Sous vide

Sous vide cooks food sealed in a bag submerged in a precisely controlled water bath, holding it at the exact final temperature you want. Because the water never exceeds the target, a steak set to 129°F cannot overcook — it reaches medium-rare edge to edge with no gray gradient, then needs only a hard sear to brown the outside. Time controls tenderness rather than doneness: a tough short rib held at a low temperature for many hours slowly converts collagen to gelatin while staying rosy. Precise temperature also transforms eggs, custards, and vegetables that would otherwise blow past their window. The tradeoffs are the lack of browning in the bath — Maillard still needs high heat afterward — and food-safety math, where lower temperatures require longer holds to pasteurize. Vacuum sealing removes insulating air so heat transfers evenly through the food.
