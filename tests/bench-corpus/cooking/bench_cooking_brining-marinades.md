# Brining and marinades

Brining and marinating are pre-cooking treatments that season and change texture, and it helps to know what each actually does. A brine — salt dissolved in water — is the powerful one: through diffusion and osmosis the salt penetrates deep and restructures the proteins so they hold more moisture, which is why a brined turkey or pork chop stays juicy. A dry brine, just salt with no water, achieves much the same with less mess and crisper skin. Marinades, by contrast, mostly flavor the surface; oil carries fat-soluble aromatics, herbs and spices cling, and an acid or enzyme from citrus, yogurt, or pineapple tenderizes only the outer few millimeters and, left too long, turns that layer mushy. So marinate for flavor over short times, and brine when you want deep, even, juicy seasoning throughout.
