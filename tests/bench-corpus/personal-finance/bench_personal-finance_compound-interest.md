# Compound interest and the saver's advantage

Compound interest is the interest your savings earn on both the original principal and on the interest already credited, so a nest egg snowballs the longer it sits untouched. A savings account or investment that compounds monthly credits earnings back into the balance, and next period those earnings themselves earn a return. The Rule of 72 gives savers a quick feel for this: divide 72 by your annual return to estimate the years your money needs to double, so a steady seven percent roughly doubles a balance every ten years. Starting to save in your twenties beats starting in your forties even with smaller contributions, because compounding rewards years in the account more than dollars added late. That is why financial planners urge households to automate contributions early, reinvest every dividend and interest payment, and leave the balance alone so the compounding curve can bend steeply upward over decades.
