# Retirement accounts: 401k and IRA basics

Tax-advantaged retirement accounts let households set aside money for later life while shielding it from taxes along the way. A workplace 401k lets you contribute a slice of each paycheck before taxes, lowering this year's taxable income, and many employers add a matching contribution that is essentially free money you should always capture in full. An IRA, opened on your own at a brokerage, offers a similar tax break with more investment freedom. The traditional flavor defers taxes until you withdraw in retirement, while the Roth flavor takes after-tax dollars now so qualified withdrawals later come out completely tax-free. Both carry annual contribution limits set by the IRS and penalties for early withdrawal before age fifty-nine and a half. The savvy sequence for most savers is to grab the full employer match first, then fund a Roth IRA, then return to max the 401k, letting decades of tax-sheltered compounding build the retirement nest egg.
