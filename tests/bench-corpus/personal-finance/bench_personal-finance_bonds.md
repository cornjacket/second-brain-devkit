# Bonds in a personal portfolio

A bond is essentially a loan you make to a government or company that pays you regular interest, called the coupon, and returns your principal on a set maturity date. For a household saver, bonds play the steadying role in a portfolio: they swing far less than stocks and deliver predictable income, which is why savers shift more toward them as retirement nears and the appetite for wild ups and downs fades. Treasury bonds backed by the government are the safest, municipal bonds offer tax-advantaged interest, and corporate bonds pay more to compensate for higher default risk. Bond prices move opposite to interest rates, so when rates rise the value of existing bonds falls, a seesaw worth understanding before you buy. Most households get their bond exposure simply through a low-cost bond index fund rather than picking individual issues, pairing it with stock funds to cushion the portfolio during market downturns while still earning steady interest.
