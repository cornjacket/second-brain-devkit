# Investing in low-cost index funds

An index fund is a pooled investment that holds every stock in a market benchmark such as the S&P 500, so a single share gives a household broad ownership of hundreds of companies at once. Because the fund simply tracks the index rather than paying managers to pick winners, its expense ratio is tiny, often under a tenth of a percent, and those saved fees compound into meaningfully larger balances over a lifetime. Decades of evidence show most actively managed funds fail to beat their benchmark after costs, which is why index investing has become the default recommendation for ordinary savers building retirement wealth. A total-market or S&P 500 index fund, held through an IRA or workplace plan and left alone through market swings, captures the long-run growth of the economy. Pair it with automatic monthly buys and you have a simple, low-maintenance core for a household portfolio.
