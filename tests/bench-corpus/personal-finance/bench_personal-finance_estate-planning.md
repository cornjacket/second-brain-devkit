# Estate planning and writing a will

Estate planning is arranging in advance how your assets and responsibilities pass to others after you die, sparing your family confusion and legal expense at a painful time. The cornerstone is a will, a legal document naming who inherits your property and, crucially for parents, who becomes guardian of minor children; without one, state law decides and a court appoints strangers to sort it out through probate. Beyond the will, households should name beneficiaries directly on retirement accounts and life insurance so those funds pass instantly outside probate, and set up a durable power of attorney and a healthcare directive so a trusted person can manage money and medical decisions if illness leaves you incapacitated. A living trust can help larger estates avoid probate delays and keep matters private. Review these documents after every major life event, such as a marriage, divorce, or new child, so your wishes stay current and your loved ones are protected.
