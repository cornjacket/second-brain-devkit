# Gauging your risk tolerance

Risk tolerance is your personal ability and willingness to watch the value of your investments swing up and down without panicking and selling at the worst moment. It has two sides: the emotional side, how well you sleep when your account drops twenty percent in a downturn, and the practical side, how soon you will need the money, since a saver decades from retirement can ride out slumps that would devastate someone withdrawing next year. Being honest about your temperament matters, because an allocation that is too aggressive tempts you to bail during a crash and lock in losses, while one too timid may not grow enough to reach your goals. A household with a long time horizon, stable income, and a solid emergency fund can shoulder more stock exposure, whereas shaky job security or a short horizon argues for more bonds and cash. Match your portfolio to the risk you can truly stomach, then stay the course.
