# Understanding home mortgages

A mortgage is a long-term loan a household uses to buy a home, secured by the property itself, and repaid in monthly installments over fifteen or thirty years. Each payment blends principal and interest, and in the early years most of it goes toward interest, so equity builds slowly before accelerating later through amortization. A larger down payment, ideally twenty percent, shrinks the loan and lets you avoid private mortgage insurance, the extra premium lenders charge when your equity cushion is thin. Fixed-rate loans lock your interest rate for the whole term, giving predictable payments, while adjustable-rate mortgages start lower but can reset higher after an introductory period. Beyond principal and interest, your monthly housing cost includes property taxes and homeowners insurance, often bundled into an escrow account. Shopping several lenders for the lowest rate, and making occasional extra principal payments, can save a household tens of thousands of dollars across the life of the loan.
