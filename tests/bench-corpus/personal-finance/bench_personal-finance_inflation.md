# How inflation erodes your savings

Inflation is the gradual rise in the prices of everyday goods and services, which quietly shrinks the purchasing power of every dollar your household holds. At a modest three percent annual rate, the groceries, gas, and rent that cost a hundred dollars today will cost more than a hundred and thirty in ten years, so cash stuffed under the mattress or left in a no-interest account steadily loses ground. This is why savers cannot simply hoard money and expect it to keep its value; the return on your savings needs to at least outpace inflation to preserve real buying power. Owning growth assets like stock index funds historically beats inflation over long horizons, and inflation-protected savings bonds adjust their payout to the cost of living. When you budget for far-off goals like a child's college or your own retirement, inflate the target so the sum you save actually covers tomorrow's higher prices rather than today's.
