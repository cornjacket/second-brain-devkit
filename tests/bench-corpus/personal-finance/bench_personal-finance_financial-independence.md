# Pursuing financial independence

Financial independence is the point at which your investments generate enough passive income to cover your living expenses, so working for a paycheck becomes a choice rather than a necessity. The popular FIRE movement, short for financial independence retire early, pursues this by saving an aggressive share of income, sometimes half or more, and investing it in low-cost index funds. A common yardstick is the rule of 25: once your portfolio reaches roughly twenty-five times your annual spending, the companion four percent rule suggests you can withdraw about four percent each year and reasonably expect the balance to last through a long retirement. The two levers are cutting expenses, which both frees up savings and lowers the number you must hit, and boosting income to save faster. Households pursuing this path track their savings rate obsessively, keep lifestyle inflation in check as earnings rise, and let compounding shrink the years to freedom, buying back their own time.
