# Common Law and Statutory Law

Two great sources supply the rules that courts apply: judge-made common law, developed incrementally through the accreted holdings of adjudicated cases, and enacted statutory law, promulgated by a legislature and codified in statutes. The common law grows by analogy and reasoned elaboration, its doctrines emerging from precedent and refined through the reasoning of successive tribunals. Statutes, by contrast, speak with legislative supremacy: a validly enacted statute displaces contrary common-law rules within its scope, and courts must give effect to the legislature's expressed will. Judges construe statutes through canons of statutory interpretation—the plain-meaning rule, in pari materia, expressio unius, and resort to legislative history—to ascertain and effectuate legislative intent. Where a statute is silent or ambiguous, the interstitial common law fills the gaps. The abrogation of a common-law doctrine by statute, and the judicial gloss that accumulates around statutory text, illustrate the continual interplay between these two doctrinal wellsprings of law.
