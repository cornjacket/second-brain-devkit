# The Rules of Evidence

The rules of evidence govern the admissibility of proof at trial, filtering what the factfinder may lawfully consider. Relevance is the threshold: evidence must have a tendency to make a material fact more or less probable, yet a court may exclude relevant proof where its probative value is substantially outweighed by unfair prejudice, confusion, or waste of time. The hearsay rule bars out-of-court statements offered for their truth, subject to numerous exceptions such as present-sense impressions, excited utterances, business records, and dying declarations. The exclusionary rule suppresses evidence obtained through unconstitutional search or seizure, and privileges shield confidential communications between attorney and client, spouses, or penitent and clergy. Authentication and the best-evidence rule condition the introduction of documents and real evidence, while lay and expert opinion testimony must satisfy foundational requirements. The trial judge, as gatekeeper, rules on objections and admissibility to ensure the reliability of the proof presented to the jury.
