# Mens Rea and Criminal Culpability

Mens rea, the guilty mind, denotes the culpable mental state that criminal law requires alongside the actus reus, the wrongful act, to establish penal liability. The Model Penal Code hierarchizes four culpable states: purposely, knowingly, recklessly, and negligently, each describing the defendant's awareness of and attitude toward the prohibited result or circumstance. Specific-intent offenses demand a heightened mental element directed at a particular consequence, whereas general-intent crimes require only the intent to perform the proscribed act. Strict-liability offenses dispense with mens rea altogether, imposing punishment upon proof of the act alone. Transferred intent redirects culpability where a defendant intends harm to one victim but injures another. The doctrine of concurrence insists that the guilty mind and the guilty act coincide in time. Mistake of fact may negate the requisite mens rea, while ignorance of the law generally furnishes no excuse to the accused wrongdoer.
