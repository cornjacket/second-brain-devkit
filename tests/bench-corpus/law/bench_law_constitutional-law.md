# Foundations of Constitutional Law

Constitutional law construes the supreme charter that establishes the structure of government and enumerates the rights of the governed. It rests on separation of powers among the legislative, executive, and judicial branches, checked and balanced against one another, and on federalism dividing sovereignty between the national government and the states. Judicial review, announced in Marbury v. Madison, empowers courts to declare statutes and executive acts void when they contravene the Constitution. The commerce clause, the supremacy clause, and the necessary-and-proper clause delimit federal legislative reach, while the Bill of Rights and the Fourteenth Amendment constrain governmental infringement of individual liberties. Courts apply tiers of scrutiny—strict, intermediate, and rational-basis—to test the constitutionality of classifications and burdens on fundamental rights. The equal protection and free speech guarantees, incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment's due process clause, anchor much of modern constitutional adjudication before the appellate bench.
