# Theories of Legal Liability

Liability denotes the legal responsibility a party bears to answer for a wrong and to satisfy the judgment a court imposes. Tort liability may rest on fault, as in negligence, on intent, as in battery or trespass, or on strict liability, which fixes responsibility without regard to fault for abnormally dangerous activities and defective products. Vicarious liability holds a principal answerable for the tortious acts of an agent committed within the scope of employment under respondeat superior. Joint and several liability permits a plaintiff to recover the entire judgment from any one of several concurrent tortfeasors, who may then seek contribution or indemnity among themselves. Contractual liability arises from breach of a bargained obligation, while statutory liability is imposed directly by legislative command. Defenses, immunities, and limitations of liability circumscribe exposure, and the apportionment of fault among culpable actors under comparative principles determines each defendant's ultimate share of the damages awarded.
