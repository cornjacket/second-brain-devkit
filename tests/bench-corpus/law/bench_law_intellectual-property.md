# The Regimes of Intellectual Property

Intellectual property law confers exclusive legal rights over intangible creations of the mind, comprising the distinct regimes of patent, copyright, trademark, and trade secret. Patent law grants an inventor a time-limited monopoly over a novel, nonobvious, and useful invention, in exchange for enabling disclosure in the claims of the patent specification. Copyright protects original works of authorship fixed in a tangible medium, vesting the author with rights of reproduction, distribution, public performance, and the creation of derivative works, subject to the fair-use defense and the idea-expression dichotomy. Trademark law safeguards distinctive marks that identify the source of goods, preventing consumer confusion and dilution, its protection turning on distinctiveness and use in commerce. Trade secret law shields confidential commercial information from misappropriation so long as the holder maintains reasonable secrecy. Infringement actions vindicate these exclusive rights, and remedies include injunctions, damages, and, in egregious cases, enhanced or statutory recoveries against the infringer.
