# Jurisdiction of Courts

Jurisdiction denotes a court's lawful authority to adjudicate a dispute and bind the parties before it. Subject-matter jurisdiction concerns the court's competence over the class of controversy: federal courts exercise limited jurisdiction grounded in federal-question or diversity jurisdiction, while state courts of general jurisdiction hear most common-law claims. Personal jurisdiction concerns the court's power over the defendant, requiring minimum contacts with the forum such that the exercise of authority comports with traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice under the International Shoe standard. General jurisdiction attaches where a defendant is at home, while specific jurisdiction arises from claims related to the defendant's forum-directed conduct. Venue rules further channel litigation to an appropriate district, and the doctrine of forum non conveniens permits dismissal to a more suitable tribunal. Long-arm statutes extend the reach of a forum's courts over nonresident defendants consistent with these constitutional due-process constraints on adjudicatory authority.
