# Consideration and the Bargained-For Exchange

Consideration is the doctrine that distinguishes an enforceable promise from a gratuitous one, requiring that each party incur a legal detriment or confer a legal benefit as the price of the other's promise. The bargained-for exchange must be sought by the promisor and given by the promisee in return, so that a past act already performed, or a moral obligation standing alone, furnishes no valid consideration. Courts do not weigh the adequacy of consideration, honoring even peppercorn recitals, but they invalidate illusory promises that reserve unfettered discretion to perform. The pre-existing duty rule denies consideration where a party merely promises to do what it was already legally bound to do. Where consideration fails, promissory estoppel may nonetheless enforce a promise upon which the promisee reasonably and detrimentally relied, invoking equity to prevent manifest injustice against the relying party's foreseeable reliance.
