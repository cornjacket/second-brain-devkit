# Elements of Valid Contract Formation

A binding contract at common law requires a valid offer, an unequivocal acceptance mirroring the offer's terms, and bargained-for consideration exchanged between the promisor and promisee. The offeror manifests a present intent to be bound, and the offeree accepts before revocation, lapse, or rejection extinguishes the power of acceptance. Under the mirror-image rule, a purported acceptance that varies material terms operates as a counteroffer rather than assent. Courts scrutinize mutual assent objectively, asking whether a reasonable person would infer agreement from the parties' outward manifestations rather than secret subjective intent. Capacity to contract, legality of the subject matter, and the absence of duress, undue influence, or fraudulent misrepresentation further condition enforceability. Where the parties intended to memorialize their bargain in a signed writing, the statute of frauds may bar enforcement of certain agreements absent a sufficient written memorandum evidencing the essential terms.
