# The Jury System and Trial by Jury

Trial by jury interposes a panel of laypersons between the state and the litigant, entrusting the finding of facts to a body of the accused's peers. The Sixth and Seventh Amendments secure the right to a jury in serious criminal prosecutions and in civil suits at common law. Prospective jurors are summoned from the venire and screened through voir dire, during which counsel probe for bias and exercise challenges for cause and a limited number of peremptory challenges, the latter constrained against discriminatory use under Batson. The empaneled petit jury weighs the evidence, receives the judge's instructions on the governing law, and deliberates in secret to reach a verdict, which in criminal cases must ordinarily be unanimous. The grand jury, a distinct body, returns indictments upon a finding of probable cause. Jury nullification, sequestration, and the prohibition against impeaching a verdict further shape the province of the jury in adjudication.
