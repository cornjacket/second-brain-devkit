# Stare Decisis and the Force of Precedent

Stare decisis, the doctrine of standing by decided matters, obliges courts to follow the holdings of prior adjudications when confronting materially similar disputes, lending predictability and stability to the common law. A precedent's binding force depends on the hierarchy of the deciding tribunal: lower courts are bound by the vertical authority of appellate and supreme courts within their jurisdiction, while a court's horizontal adherence to its own past rulings is strong but rebuttable. The ratio decidendi, the essential legal reasoning necessary to the judgment, binds future courts, whereas obiter dicta, incidental observations, carry only persuasive weight. Litigants distinguish adverse precedent by identifying material factual differences, and courts may overrule decisions rendered unworkable, eroded by later doctrine, or wrongly reasoned. The interplay of binding and persuasive authority, and the reasoned elaboration of holdings across successive cases, constitutes the incremental method by which judge-made law evolves within a stable doctrinal framework.
