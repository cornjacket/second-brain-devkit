# The Tort of Negligence

Negligence is the tort that imposes liability when a defendant breaches a duty of reasonable care owed to a foreseeable plaintiff, thereby proximately causing legally cognizable harm. The plaintiff must establish four elements: duty, breach, causation, and damages. Duty arises from the reasonable-person standard, measuring the defendant's conduct against that of a prudent actor under like circumstances. Breach occurs when conduct falls below that standard, sometimes proven by res ipsa loquitur where the injury ordinarily bespeaks negligence. Causation demands both actual cause under the but-for test and proximate cause limiting liability to foreseeable consequences within the scope of the risk. Contributory or comparative negligence, assumption of risk, and intervening superseding causes operate as defenses that diminish or defeat recovery. The tortfeasor's negligent breach must be the legal cause of the claimant's compensable injury before a court will award damages for the resulting loss.
