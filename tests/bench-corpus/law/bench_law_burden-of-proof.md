# Burdens and Standards of Proof

The burden of proof allocates the risk of nonpersuasion between the parties to litigation and fixes the quantum of evidence a proponent must marshal to prevail. It comprises two components: the burden of production, requiring a party to adduce sufficient evidence to place a fact in issue and avoid a directed verdict, and the burden of persuasion, requiring the factfinder to be convinced to the applicable standard. Three principal standards govern: the preponderance of the evidence, the more-likely-than-not threshold that ordinarily controls civil suits; clear and convincing evidence, an intermediate standard for claims such as fraud; and proof beyond a reasonable doubt, the exacting standard the prosecution must satisfy in a criminal trial. The burden generally rests upon the party asserting the affirmative of an issue, though rebuttable presumptions and affirmative defenses may shift the burden of production to the opposing litigant during the course of adjudication.
