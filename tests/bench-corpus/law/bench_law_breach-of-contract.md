# Breach of Contract and Its Consequences

A breach of contract occurs when a party fails, without lawful excuse, to perform a duty owed under the terms of a binding agreement. Breaches range from a minor or partial failure, which entitles the aggrieved party to damages but not to suspend its own performance, to a material breach that discharges the nonbreaching party's remaining obligations and permits rescission. An anticipatory repudiation, by which a promisor unequivocally disavows performance before it falls due, gives the promisee an immediate right of action. The nonbreaching party must mitigate its losses, and expectation damages measured by the benefit of the bargain aim to place it in the position full performance would have secured, subject to the limits of foreseeability under Hadley v. Baxendale and reasonable certainty. Reliance and restitution interests supply alternative measures. Doctrines of substantial performance, conditions precedent, and impossibility or frustration of purpose may excuse or qualify the promisor's obligation to perform.
