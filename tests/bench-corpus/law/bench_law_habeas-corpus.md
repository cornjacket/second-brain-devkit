# The Writ of Habeas Corpus

Habeas corpus, the great writ of liberty, commands a custodian to produce a detained prisoner before a court and justify the legality of the detention. Rooted in the common law and enshrined in the Constitution's suspension clause, the writ tests whether a person is held in violation of the law, furnishing a collateral remedy against unlawful imprisonment. A convicted prisoner who has exhausted direct appeals may petition for the writ, challenging the constitutionality of the conviction or sentence on grounds such as ineffective assistance of counsel, prosecutorial suppression of exculpatory evidence, or jurisdictional defect. Federal habeas review of state judgments is cabined by doctrines of procedural default, exhaustion of state remedies, and deference to reasonable state-court adjudications under the governing statute. The suspension clause permits withdrawal of the privilege only in cases of rebellion or invasion when the public safety requires it, underscoring the writ's centrality to safeguarding personal liberty against arbitrary executive confinement.
