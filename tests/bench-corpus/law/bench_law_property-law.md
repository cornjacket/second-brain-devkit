# Estates and Interests in Property Law

Property law governs the rights, interests, and estates that persons may hold in land and chattels. The fee simple absolute is the largest estate, conveying perpetual and inheritable ownership, while lesser freehold estates include the life estate and the defeasible fees subject to conditions subsequent or executory limitations. Future interests—reversions, remainders, and executory interests—allocate ownership across time, constrained by the rule against perpetuities. Concurrent ownership arises through tenancy in common, joint tenancy with its right of survivorship, and tenancy by the entirety. Nonpossessory interests such as easements, profits, covenants, and equitable servitudes burden and benefit parcels of land, running with the estate to bind successors. Adverse possession may ripen open, notorious, hostile, and continuous occupation into title. Conveyancing operates through deeds, recording statutes resolve competing claims among grantees, and the landlord-tenant relationship creates leasehold estates governing possession between lessor and lessee.
