# Appellate Review and the Appeals Process

The appellate process permits a party aggrieved by a trial court's judgment to seek review before a higher tribunal. The appellant files a notice of appeal, and the appellate court examines the record below for reversible error, confined ordinarily to issues preserved through timely objection and not waived. Standards of review calibrate the deference owed the lower court: questions of law are reviewed de novo, findings of fact for clear error, and discretionary rulings for abuse of discretion. Harmless-error analysis spares a judgment where the mistake did not affect substantial rights, while plain-error review reaches unpreserved defects of exceptional gravity. The appellate panel may affirm, reverse, vacate, or remand for further proceedings, articulating its reasoning in a written opinion that may itself become binding precedent. Discretionary review by a court of last resort proceeds through a writ of certiorari, granted selectively to resolve conflicts among the intermediate appellate courts or questions of broad importance.
