# Distinguishing Criminal and Civil Actions

Criminal and civil litigation differ in their parties, purposes, and burdens of persuasion. A criminal prosecution is brought by the sovereign against an accused defendant to vindicate public order, punishing culpable conduct through incarceration, fines, or probation, and requiring proof beyond a reasonable doubt. A civil action, by contrast, is instituted by a private plaintiff seeking redress against a defendant, ordinarily to recover damages or obtain injunctive relief, proven merely by a preponderance of the evidence. The criminal accused enjoys constitutional safeguards absent in civil suits: the privilege against self-incrimination, the right to counsel, protection against double jeopardy, and the presumption of innocence. The same wrongful act may spawn both a prosecution and a tort suit, since acquittal under the heavier criminal standard does not preclude civil liability under the lighter standard. The remedies, procedural protections, and evidentiary thresholds thus diverge sharply between the two adjudicative tracks.
