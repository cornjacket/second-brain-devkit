# Teaching subtext

Subtext — the meaning running underneath the spoken lines — is what makes dialogue feel like real human behavior, and it must be taught because beginners tend to play the literal words. Teachers show that people rarely say exactly what they mean, so an actor's job is to find what the character actually wants and is hiding beneath the text, letting the tension between the said and the unsaid drive the scene. Exercises include speaking the subtext aloud in improvised paraphrase, or playing a loving line with hostile intent, so students feel how intention colors identical words. Coaches tie subtext to objective and tactic: a line is a move in a strategy, not a statement of fact. When acting feels flat, the diagnosis is often that the actor is playing the words instead of the intention beneath them.
