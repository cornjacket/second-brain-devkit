# Teaching emotional preparation

Emotional preparation is the work an actor does in the wings so they enter a scene already in the right state, and teachers treat it as craft rather than mood-hoping. The lesson is that a character often walks in mid-life — furious, grieving, elated — and playing that from a cold start reads false, so the actor uses a private preparation, an image, a substitution, or a sensory memory, to arrive already charged. Coaches stress that preparation only gets the actor to the doorway; once the scene begins, attention shifts to the partner and the objective, and the prepared feeling is released, not clung to. Overdone, it makes actors self-absorbed and disconnected. Done well, it means the first line already carries a full inner life instead of the actor warming up on stage.
