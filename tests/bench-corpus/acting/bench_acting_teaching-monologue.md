# Coaching a monologue

Coaching a monologue teaches an actor to sustain a piece alone, and teachers approach it as a scene with an unseen partner rather than a recitation. The first questions are who the character is talking to, what they want from that listener, and why they must speak now — because a monologue is still an attempt to affect someone, not a display of feelings. Coaches help students map the shifts of thought so the speech moves through discoveries and tactics instead of running at one level. They watch for the trap of generalized emotion and push for specific images and stakes. Practical notes cover choosing a piece that fits the actor, honoring the punctuation, and finding a strong opening moment. A well-coached monologue feels like spontaneous, active thought rather than memorized, pre-shaped text.
