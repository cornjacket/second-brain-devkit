# Teaching status

Status work, popularized by Keith Johnstone, teaches that every interaction is a negotiation of high and low status, and it gives actors a concrete, playable tool. Students learn that status is something you do, not something you are — played through eye contact, stillness, how much space you take, the steadiness of the voice, and whether you finish your sentences. Teachers run exercises where actors play a scene at various status levels, or where one character keeps raising or lowering their status, revealing how the dynamic drives comedy and conflict. The lesson is that clashing or shifting status makes a scene come alive, and that a character who raises themselves by lowering others is instantly readable. It gives students an external, physical handle on relationships that reaching for pure emotion does not.
