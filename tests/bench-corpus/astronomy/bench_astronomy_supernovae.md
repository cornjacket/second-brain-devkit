# Supernovae

A supernova is the cataclysmic explosion that ends a star's life, briefly outshining an entire galaxy and dispersing newly forged elements across interstellar space. Core-collapse supernovae, classified as Type II, occur when a massive star exhausts its nuclear fuel and its iron core implodes, rebounding in a shock wave that blasts the outer envelope outward while the core becomes a neutron star or black hole. Thermonuclear supernovae, Type Ia, arise when a white dwarf in a binary accretes matter until it nears the Chandrasekhar limit and detonates in a runaway carbon fusion, leaving no remnant. Because Type Ia events share a consistent peak luminosity, astronomers wield them as standard candles to measure cosmic distances. Supernovae seed galaxies with the heavy elements essential for planets and life, and their expanding remnants sculpt the interstellar medium and trigger new episodes of star formation.
