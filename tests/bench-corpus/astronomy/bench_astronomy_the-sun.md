# The Sun

The Sun is the G-type main-sequence star at the center of our Solar System, a vast sphere of hot plasma that fuses roughly six hundred million tons of hydrogen into helium every second in its core through the proton-proton chain. Energy generated in the core migrates outward slowly through the radiative zone, then churns through the convective zone before escaping from the visible surface, the photosphere, at about 5,800 kelvin. Above it lie the tenuous chromosphere and the searingly hot corona, which streams charged particles outward as the solar wind. The Sun's magnetic field, tangled by differential rotation, drives an eleven-year cycle of sunspots, solar flares, and coronal mass ejections that spark auroras and geomagnetic storms on Earth. Having shone for 4.6 billion years, the Sun will eventually exhaust its core hydrogen, swell into a red giant, and shed its outer layers to leave a slowly cooling white dwarf.
