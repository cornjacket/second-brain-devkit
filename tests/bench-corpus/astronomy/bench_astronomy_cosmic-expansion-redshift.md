# Cosmic Expansion and Redshift

Cosmic expansion is the observed stretching of space itself, first quantified when Edwin Hubble found that distant galaxies recede at velocities proportional to their distance, a relation encoded in the Hubble constant. As a galaxy's light travels across expanding space, its wavelengths are stretched toward longer, redder values, a cosmological redshift distinct from the Doppler shift of local motion. The greater a galaxy's redshift, the farther away and further back in time we observe it. Late-twentieth-century measurements of distant Type Ia supernovae revealed that the expansion is not slowing but accelerating, driven by a mysterious dark energy that behaves like a cosmological constant permeating the vacuum. Extrapolating the expansion backward points to the hot dense beginning of the Big Bang, while the balance between matter, dark energy, and spatial curvature governs the ultimate fate of the cosmos, whether it expands forever or eventually reverses.
