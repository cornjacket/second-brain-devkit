# The Big Bang

The Big Bang is the prevailing cosmological model describing the origin of the universe from an extremely hot, dense state some 13.8 billion years ago, after which space itself began expanding and cooling. In the first fraction of a second a period of inflation stretched quantum fluctuations into the seeds of cosmic structure. As the universe cooled, quarks bound into protons and neutrons, and within the first few minutes primordial nucleosynthesis forged the light elements hydrogen, helium, and traces of lithium in abundances that closely match observation. About 380,000 years later, the universe became transparent as electrons combined with nuclei, releasing the cosmic microwave background radiation that we detect today as a nearly uniform afterglow at 2.7 kelvin. Tiny temperature anisotropies in this relic radiation encode the geometry and composition of the cosmos, providing powerful evidence that the observable universe emerged from a single primeval expansion.
