# Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion

Kepler's three laws of planetary motion, derived from Tycho Brahe's meticulous observations of Mars, describe how planets orbit the Sun. The first law states that each planet travels along an ellipse with the Sun at one focus, replacing the ancient assumption of perfect circles and explaining the varying distance between perihelion and aphelion. The second law, the law of equal areas, holds that a line joining a planet to the Sun sweeps out equal areas in equal times, so a planet moves fastest near perihelion and slowest at aphelion, a consequence of conserved angular momentum. The third law relates orbital period to distance, stating that the square of a planet's period is proportional to the cube of its semi-major axis. Newton later showed these empirical laws follow from his law of universal gravitation, and astronomers still apply them to satellites, moons, and distant exoplanetary systems.
