# The Hertzsprung-Russell Diagram

The Hertzsprung-Russell diagram plots stellar luminosity against surface temperature or spectral type, revealing that stars are not scattered randomly but occupy distinct regions tied to their evolutionary state. Most stars fall along the diagonal main sequence, where hot, luminous blue O and B stars sit at the upper left and cool, faint red M dwarfs at the lower right, each fusing hydrogen in its core. Above the main sequence lie the red giants and supergiants, cool yet enormously luminous because of their vast radii, while the hot but dim white dwarfs cluster at the lower left. A star's position encodes its mass, temperature, and radius through the Stefan-Boltzmann relation. Astronomers use the diagram to gauge cluster ages, since the main-sequence turnoff point marks where the most massive stars have already evolved off toward the giant branch.
