# Galaxies

A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter, ranging from dwarf galaxies with a few million stars to giants containing hundreds of billions. Edwin Hubble classified galaxies by morphology into spirals with sweeping arms of young blue stars and ongoing star formation, ellipticals dominated by old red stars and little gas, and irregulars lacking clear structure. Most massive galaxies harbor a supermassive black hole at their nucleus, and active galactic nuclei blaze as quasars when that black hole feeds voraciously. Galaxies cluster into groups and clusters bound by gravity, arranged along filaments and walls that thread the cosmic web separated by vast voids. Galaxies grow through mergers, and interacting pairs stretch tidal tails of stars across intergalactic space. Rotation curves that stay flat far from the center reveal that each galaxy is embedded in an extended halo of dark matter.
