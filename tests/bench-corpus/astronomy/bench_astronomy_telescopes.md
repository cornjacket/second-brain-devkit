# Telescopes

A telescope gathers and focuses light to reveal celestial objects far too faint or distant for the unaided eye. Refracting telescopes bend light with a lens, while reflecting telescopes collect it with a curved primary mirror, an approach that scales to the enormous apertures of modern observatories because larger mirrors gather more photons and resolve finer detail. Resolving power improves with aperture and shortens with wavelength, though Earth's turbulent atmosphere blurs images until adaptive optics flex a deformable mirror to correct the distortion in real time. Astronomers observe across the electromagnetic spectrum, deploying radio dishes, infrared, ultraviolet, and X-ray instruments, many lofted above the atmosphere as space telescopes like Hubble and James Webb. Radio interferometers combine widely separated dishes to synthesize the resolution of a single giant aperture. Spectrographs split incoming starlight into its component wavelengths, letting astronomers read the composition, temperature, and motion of distant stars and galaxies.
