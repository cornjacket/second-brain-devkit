# Gravitational Lensing

Gravitational lensing is the bending of light as it passes through the curved spacetime around a massive object, a prediction of Einstein's general relativity first confirmed during the 1919 solar eclipse when starlight grazing the Sun appeared displaced. When a massive galaxy or cluster lies between us and a more distant source, its gravity deflects the background light, magnifying, distorting, or multiplying the source's image. Strong lensing produces dramatic arcs and, when the alignment is nearly perfect, complete Einstein rings, and it can even split a distant quasar into several images. Weak lensing subtly shears the shapes of countless background galaxies, letting astronomers map the distribution of unseen dark matter across the sky. Microlensing, a brief brightening as a foreground star crosses in front of a background one, has revealed exoplanets and compact dark objects. Lensing thus serves as a natural telescope, magnifying the faintest, most distant galaxies in the early universe.
