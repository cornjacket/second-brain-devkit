# Dark Matter

Dark matter is an invisible form of mass that neither emits nor absorbs light yet makes up roughly a quarter of the universe's total energy content, vastly outweighing ordinary luminous matter. Its presence was inferred when Vera Rubin measured galactic rotation curves that stay flat far from the center, implying far more gravitating mass than the visible stars can supply. On larger scales, the orbital speeds of galaxies within clusters and the gravitational lensing of background light both demand an unseen halo of matter. The Bullet Cluster, where lensing mass is offset from the hot X-ray gas after a collision, offers striking evidence that dark matter is distinct from normal baryons. Leading candidates are weakly interacting massive particles or axions that interact only through gravity. Dark matter provided the gravitational scaffolding on which galaxies and the cosmic web assembled, and its imprint appears in the cosmic microwave background.
