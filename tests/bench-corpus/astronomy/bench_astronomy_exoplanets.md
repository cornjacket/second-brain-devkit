# Exoplanets

An exoplanet is a planet orbiting a star beyond our Sun, and thousands have been confirmed since the first was found around a Sun-like star in 1995. The transit method detects the tiny periodic dimming as a planet crosses its star's disk, a technique the Kepler and TESS missions used to survey vast fields of stars. The radial velocity method senses the star's slight wobble as an orbiting planet tugs it back and forth, shifting its spectral lines. Discovered worlds span hot Jupiters skimming their stars, mini-Neptunes, and rocky super-Earths, some orbiting within the habitable zone where liquid water could persist on the surface. Astronomers probe exoplanet atmospheres through transmission spectroscopy, reading absorption features imprinted as starlight filters through, and the James Webb Space Telescope now detects molecules like water and carbon dioxide, sharpening the search for potentially habitable worlds and biosignatures around other stars.
