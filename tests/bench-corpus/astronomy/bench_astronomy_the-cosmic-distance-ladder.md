# The Cosmic Distance Ladder

The cosmic distance ladder is the sequence of overlapping techniques astronomers use to measure distances across the universe, each rung calibrating the next. The lowest rung is parallax, the tiny apparent shift of a nearby star against distant background stars as Earth orbits the Sun, giving direct geometric distances in parsecs. For farther reaches, astronomers use standard candles, objects of known intrinsic luminosity whose apparent brightness reveals their distance. Cepheid variable stars pulsate with periods tied to their luminosity, extending the ladder to nearby galaxies, while Type Ia supernovae, all detonating at nearly the same peak brightness, reach across billions of light-years. At the greatest scales, a galaxy's redshift combined with the Hubble constant yields its distance. Each rung inherits and compounds the calibration of the rungs below, and refining these standard candles remains central to pinning down the expansion rate and age of the cosmos.
