# The Stellar Life Cycle

A star is born when a dense core within a giant molecular cloud collapses under gravity, heating until hydrogen fusion ignites and the protostar settles onto the main sequence. There it spends most of its life fusing hydrogen into helium in its core, balancing gravitational contraction against radiation pressure in hydrostatic equilibrium. When core hydrogen is exhausted, the star swells into a red giant, fusing helium into carbon and, in massive stars, forging progressively heavier elements through successive burning shells up to iron. A low-mass star like the Sun sheds its envelope as a planetary nebula, leaving a white dwarf that slowly cools. A massive star's iron core cannot fuse further, so it collapses catastrophically and detonates as a supernova, leaving behind a neutron star or black hole. Stellar mass thus dictates luminosity, lifespan, and ultimate fate.
