# The Moon and Tides

The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite, most likely formed when a Mars-sized body struck the young Earth and flung debris into orbit that coalesced into our companion. It is tidally locked, rotating once per orbit so that the same near side always faces Earth while the far side remains hidden, and its cratered highlands and dark basaltic maria record billions of years of impacts. The Moon's phases, from new to full, arise as the geometry of Sun, Earth, and Moon changes the fraction of the illuminated hemisphere we see, and eclipses occur when the three align. The Moon's gravity raises tidal bulges in Earth's oceans, producing two high tides daily, while the Sun's weaker pull amplifies them into spring tides and moderates them into neap tides. Tidal friction gradually slows Earth's rotation and pushes the Moon steadily outward by a few centimeters each year.
