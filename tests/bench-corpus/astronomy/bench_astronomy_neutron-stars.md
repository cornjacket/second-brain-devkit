# Neutron Stars

A neutron star is the collapsed core left behind by a massive star's supernova, packing more than the Sun's mass into a sphere only about twenty kilometers across, so dense that a sugar-cube of its material would weigh billions of tons. Gravity crushes protons and electrons together into neutrons, supported against further collapse by neutron degeneracy pressure. Conservation of angular momentum spins these remnants hundreds of times per second, and their intense magnetic fields channel beams of radiation from the magnetic poles. When such a beam sweeps across Earth, we detect a pulsar, ticking with clocklike regularity. The most magnetized neutron stars, magnetars, unleash violent bursts of gamma rays. When two neutron stars in a binary spiral together and merge, they emit gravitational waves and forge heavy elements like gold and platinum in a kilonova, enriching the galaxy with the products of rapid neutron capture.
