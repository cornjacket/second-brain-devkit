# Spaced repetition

Spaced repetition is a review scheduling technique that exploits the spacing effect and the forgetting curve: each flashcard resurfaces at an expanding interval timed to just before you would forget it, so every recall reconsolidates the memory and pushes the next review further out. The recall must be active — you retrieve the answer from memory before flipping the card, not merely reread it — which is what encodes durable, long-term retention. Scheduling algorithms like SM-2 (Anki's SuperMemo-derived engine) and the newer FSRS adjust each card's interval and ease from your graded response, from "again" for a lapse to "easy" for effortless recall. In a PKM practice this closes the loop between notes and memory: you author atomic prompts from your evergreen notes, often via plugins that turn `Question :: Answer` lines or highlighted vault text into a deck, so the knowledge you captured becomes recallable rather than a write-only archive you never revisit.
